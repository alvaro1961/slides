Abstract Vector Spaces

Definition

A vector space \(V\) over \(\mathbb{R}\) is a set with:

  • Vector addition (\(+\)):

    For \(\mathbf{v},\mathbf{w}\in V\), \(\mathbf{v}+\mathbf{w}\) is well defined and it is in \(V\).

  • Scalar multiplication (\(\cdot\))

    For \(\mathbf{v}\in V\) and \(c\in\mathbb{R}\), \(c\mathbf{v}\) is well defined and it is in \(V\).

  • The operations satisfy certain properties

Vector Space Properties

  • Commutativity and associativity of addition \[\begin{aligned} \mathbf{v}+\mathbf{w} &= \mathbf{w}+\mathbf{v}\\ (\mathbf{v}+\mathbf{w})+\mathbf{z} &= \mathbf{v}+(\mathbf{w}+\mathbf{z})\\ \end{aligned} \]

  • Existence of zero vector and additive inverse

    • There exists \(\mathbf{0}\in V\) such that for every \(\mathbf{v}\in V\) \[\mathbf{v}+\mathbf{0} = \mathbf{v}\]
    • For every \(\mathbf{v}\in V\) there exists \(-\mathbf{v}\in V\) such that \[\mathbf{v}+(-\mathbf{v})=\mathbf{0}\]
  • Distributivity properties \[\begin{aligned} (a+b)\mathbf{v}&=a\mathbf{v}+b\mathbf{v}\\ a(\mathbf{v}+\mathbf{w}) &= a\mathbf{v}+a\mathbf{w} \end{aligned} \]
  • Associativity of the scalar product \[(ab)\mathbf{v}=a(b\mathbf{v})\]
  • Identity element of scalar multiplication \[1\mathbf{v}=\mathbf{v}\]

Examples: \(\mathbb{R}^n\)

  • Vectors: \[\mathbf{u} = (u_1, \ldots, u_n),\quad \mathbf{v} = (v_1, \ldots, v_n)\]
  • Addition: \[\mathbf{u} + \mathbf{v} = (u_1 + v_1, \ldots, u_n + v_n)\]
  • Scalar multiplication: For \(c \in \mathbb{R}\), \[c \mathbf{u} = (c u_1, \ldots, c u_n)\]

Examples: Function Spaces

  • \(C[a,b]\): continuous functions on \([a,b]\)
  • Addition: \[(f+g)(x) = f(x) + g(x)\]
  • Scalar multiplication: \[(cf)(x) = c f(x)$, $c \in \mathbb{R}\]

Recall from calculus 1 that

  • The sum of two continuous function is continuous, and
  • The product of a scalar with a continuous function is continuous

Examples: Polynomial Spaces

  • \(\mathbb{P}_n\): polynomials of degree \(\leq n\)
  • Vectors: \[\begin{aligned} p(x)&=a_0+a_1x+a_2x^2+\cdots+a_nx^n\\ q(x)&=b_0+b_1x+b_2x^2+\cdots+b_nx^n \end{aligned}\]
  • Addition: \[ (p+q)(x)=(a_0+b_0)+(a_1+b_1)x+(a_2+b_2)x^2+\cdots+(a_n+b_n)x^n \]
  • Scalar multiplication: For \(c\in\mathbb{R}\) \[ (cp)(x)=(ca_0)+(ca_1)x+(ca_2)x^2+\cdots+(ca_n)x^n \]

Subspaces

Definition of Subspaces

A subset \(W\) of the vector space \(V\) is called a subspace if:

  1. The zero vector \(\mathbf{0}\) is in \(W\)
  2. For all \(\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v}\in W\), \(\mathbf{u}+\mathbf{v}\) is in \(W\)
  3. For all \(\mathbf{v}\in W\) and \(c\in\mathbb{R}\), \(c\mathbf{v}\) is in \(W\)

Main Theorem:

Every subspace of a vector space \(V\) is itself a vector space.

Proof Strategy

To prove this, we need to:

  • Show these operations are well-defined on \(W\)
    • This follows from subspace properties 2 and 3.
  • Verify all vector space properties
    • This follows from the fact that operations are inherited from \(V\)

Vector Addition Properties

  1. Commutativity: For \(\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v}\in W\)

    • \(\mathbf{u}+\mathbf{v}=\mathbf{v}+\mathbf{u}\) (inherited from \(V\))

  2. Associativity: For \(\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v},\mathbf{w}\in W\)

    • \((\mathbf{u}+\mathbf{v})+\mathbf{w}=\mathbf{u}+(\mathbf{v}+\mathbf{w})\) (inherited from \(V\))

  3. Zero vector: Given by subspace property 1

  4. Additive inverse: For \(\mathbf{v}\in W\)

    • \((-1)\mathbf{v}\in W\) by scalar multiplication property
    • \(\mathbf{v}+(-1)\mathbf{v}=\mathbf{0}\)

Scalar Multiplication Properties

  1. Distributivity over vector addition:
    • \(a(\mathbf{u}+\mathbf{v})=a\mathbf{u}+a\mathbf{v}\) (inherited from \(V\))
  2. Distributivity over scalar addition:
    • \((a+b)\mathbf{v}=a\mathbf{v}+b\mathbf{v}\) (inherited from \(V\))
  3. Associativity:
    • \(a(b\mathbf{v})=(ab)\mathbf{v}\) (inherited from \(V\))
  4. Identity:
    • \(1\mathbf{v}=\mathbf{v}\) (inherited from \(V\))

Example: Subspaces of \(\mathbb{R}^n\)

Span as a Subspace

Given vectors \(\mathbf{v}_1,\ldots,\mathbf{v}_k\in\mathbb{R}^n\), their span is:

\[\text{span}\{\mathbf{v}_1,\ldots,\mathbf{v}_k\}=\{c_1\mathbf{v}_1+\cdots+c_k\mathbf{v}_k:c_1,\ldots,c_k\in\mathbb{R}\}\]

This is the set of all linear combinations of the vectors.

Why is Span a Subspace?

Letâ€™s verify the three subspace properties:

  1. Zero vector:
    • Take all \(c_i=0\)
    • Then \(0\mathbf{v}_1+\cdots+0\mathbf{v}_k=\mathbf{0}\)
  2. Closed under addition:
    • Take two vectors in span: \(\sum c_i\mathbf{v}_i\) and \(\sum d_i\mathbf{v}_i\)
    • Their sum: \(\sum (c_i+d_i)\mathbf{v}_i\) is still in span

Why is Span a Subspace? (cont.)

  1. Closed under scalar multiplication:
    • Take vector in span: \(\sum c_i\mathbf{v}_i\)
    • Scalar multiple: \(a(\sum c_i\mathbf{v}_i)=\sum (ac_i)\mathbf{v}_i\)
    • Still a linear combination, so still in span

Inner Product Spaces: Definition

An inner product on a vector space \(V\) is a function \(\langle\cdot,\cdot\rangle:V\times V\to\mathbb{R}\) satisfying:

  1. Symmetry: For all \(\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v}\in V\) \[\langle\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v}\rangle=\langle\mathbf{v},\mathbf{u}\rangle\]

  2. Linearity in first argument: For all \(\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v},\mathbf{w}\in V\) and \(c\in\mathbb{R}\) \[\begin{aligned} \langle\mathbf{u}+\mathbf{v},\mathbf{w}\rangle &= \langle\mathbf{u},\mathbf{w}\rangle+\langle\mathbf{v},\mathbf{w}\rangle\\ \langle c\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v}\rangle &= c\langle\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v}\rangle \end{aligned}\]

  3. Positive definiteness: For all \(\mathbf{v}\in V\) \[\langle\mathbf{v},\mathbf{v}\rangle\geq 0\text{ and }\langle\mathbf{v},\mathbf{v}\rangle=0\text{ if and only if }\mathbf{v}=\mathbf{0}\]

Consequences

  • Linearity in second argument follows from symmetry
  • Cauchy-Schwarz inequality holds
  • Induces a norm: \(\|\mathbf{v}\|=\sqrt{\langle\mathbf{v},\mathbf{v}\rangle}\)

Example: Polynomial Space

Inner product on \(\mathbb{P}_n\):

\[\langle p,q\rangle=\int_{-1}^1 p(x)q(x)\,dx\]

  • Symmetry: \(\int_{-1}^1 p(x)q(x)\,dx=\int_{-1}^1 q(x)p(x)\,dx\)
  • Linearity follows from integral properties
  • Positive definite: \(\int_{-1}^1 [p(x)]^2\,dx\geq 0\)

Example: \(\mathbb{R}^n\)

Standard inner product:

\[\langle\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v}\rangle=\sum_{i=1}^n u_iv_i\]

  • Familiar dot product
  • Symmetry from commutativity of multiplication
  • Linearity from distributivity
  • Sum of squares is positive

Other Inner Products

  • Weighted inner product on \(\mathbb{R}^n\): \[\langle\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v}\rangle_W=\sum_{i=1}^n w_iu_iv_i,\quad w_i>0\]

  • On \(C[a,b]\): \[\langle f,g\rangle=\int_a^b f(x)g(x)\,dx\]

Summary

  • Abstract vector spaces unify diverse mathematical objects
  • Defined by vector addition and scalar multiplication
  • Subspaces inherit vector space structure
  • Inner products provide geometric tools
